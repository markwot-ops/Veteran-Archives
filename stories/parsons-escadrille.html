Holyoke in the Great War · A Hero's Story

The Holyoke Man Who Flew for France

Edwin C. Parsons — Lafayette Escadrille, 1917

America was not yet in the war. On January 24, 1917 — months before his country declared anything — a Holyoke man named Edwin Parsons joined the Lafayette Escadrille, the French squadron flown by American volunteers. He was one of thirty-eight who went.

They flew for France because France was the one fighting. There was no draft board that sent them, no order that compelled them, no flag of their own over the aerodrome. They were private citizens of a neutral country who decided the war was theirs anyway, and got into airplanes made of wood and fabric to prove it.

Over the course of his service, Parsons downed eight German planes.

Eight. In an aircraft with no parachute, no armor, and an engine that could seize without warning over the German lines. The Escadrille's American volunteers became famous, and Parsons was among the small number who survived long enough to be called an ace and to come home.

What he did afterward reads like three more lives. He joined the FBI as a special agent. He went out to Hollywood and worked as a technical director on motion pictures — the men who made the flying pictures needed somebody who had actually done the flying, and Ted Parsons had. He served in the Navy in the Second World War and rose to Rear Admiral. He died in Osprey, Florida, in 1968.

Holyoke sent 3,797 men and women into the First World War — perhaps the highest percentage of any city its size in the United States. One of them got there first, in a French uniform, before the country he came from had made up its mind.

Research note: the city's published record names him both as Edwin C. Parsons and as Edward C. Parsons, and the same account lists him among Holyoke's men cited for bravery. It credits him as technical director for Wings, describing it as a book about the exploits of the Escadrille; Wings is better known as a 1927 motion picture, and Parsons himself wrote an account of his Escadrille service. His middle initial, the exact titles, and his flying record all await confirmation against the squadron records and his own published writing. He is carried here so he is not lost while that work is done.
Source: Holyoke's published record of the First World War.
