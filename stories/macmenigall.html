Corporal MacMenigall's Story

Cpl. Roy MacMenigall, Company D, 104th Infantry — Château-Thierry and capture, 1918

Corporal Roy MacMenigall of Company D, 104th Infantry — "Holyoke's Own" — gave this vivid, first-person account of the fighting at Château-Thierry and of his capture by the Germans. His story follows:

After our company left the Toul sector to go to Château-Thierry the boys thought they were going to Paris for a rest. They were put in box cars and started in that direction and got near enough to see the city when they were switched to one side and stopped at Noissy le Sec, then started off again to a large farm where D Company was billeted about four days and enjoyed a good rest. Then came the order to move, and the company was loaded on auto trucks about 5 a.m. on July 3 and rode all day and into the evening. As we went the sound of the guns grew louder and louder. The French lined the roads and wished us good luck, saying we were going into a bad sector.

Our company took over the positions of a company of Marines in a ravine which resembled the Elmwood dingle, only not so deep; at the bottom a brook flowed. On the sides next the brook were dugouts — merely holes in the bank, good against shrapnel but not against a shell. On arrival, work had to be started burying the dead Germans and Marines lying in the ravine. The front line was pushed out into a wheat field, where the boys dug in like graves and covered themselves with wheat, and in some places set up small crosses so German aviators would take the line for a graveyard. It worked; the enemy shelled the edge of the ravine and let the wheat field alone.

My duty as corporal of the runners was to stand at the major's dugout and, when he called for a runner, go and fetch one — four hours on and eight off. All the while the German shells came over, and to protect myself I stood in the dugout entrance. It seemed cruel to send the runners into the shell fire, where their chance of life was small, but it had to be done, and those runners were among the bravest. We wanted to go together, not alone, and would join hands and run double on our errands. I saw Jack Lambert meet his death there, and a bugler and five or six others.

Later, through a mix-up in orders, the company got into position late and was shelled and gassed. Machine guns in front fired directly at us, and as we advanced the Germans made a run for a wooded hill. A number of the boys were hit. I lay flat in the wheat while a sniper fired at me. A little way off I could hear Mason singing "Tipperary," and another man call out that he was shot. The last I remember, the Germans were shelling the woods; when I came to, a German was pushing me with his foot, and others stood around, so I knew the game was up. He asked if I was wounded and I told him no. He handed me a cigarette, took my pistol, and said, "Come."

He asked where our line was and I told him we had none — that we were chasing them as they retreated. Later he said he was himself an American, caught in Germany when the war began and pressed into service. That night we were sent to regimental headquarters and slept in a barn with 200 Germans; a shell hit the barn and killed thirteen of them. On the way to the rear the Allied planes came over and smashed the artillery in the woods. The food was poor until we reached Germany and the Red Cross rations arrived. The prisoners were kept until the Armistice, when the Germans threw open the gates and told them to go home.

Source: Charles S. Zack, Holyoke in the Great War (1919).
