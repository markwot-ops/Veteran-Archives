Holyoke in the Great War · A Hero's Story

Chief Boatswain's Mate John MacKenzie — Medal of Honor

U.S.S. Remlik, Bay of Biscay, December 17, 1917 — Holyoke's only Medal of Honor recipient

John MacKenzie is the only Medal of Honor recipient buried in Holyoke. He earned the nation's highest decoration for a single act aboard the U.S.S. Remlik in the Bay of Biscay — recounted here from Zack's history and the official Navy record:

MacKenzie was a veteran of the sea long before the war. He served as a naval apprentice from 1902 to 1907, in which time he logged more than 66,000 miles, then left the Navy for the automobile-accessory business in Springfield. He re-entered the naval service on May 12, 1917, and went overseas on convoy duty and submarine chasing, rated a Chief Boatswain's Mate aboard the U.S.S. Remlik — a converted yacht pressed into war work.

On December 17, 1917, the Remlik was convoying in the Bay of Biscay, a day and a half into a hundred-mile gale, and had just sighted a submarine. In MacKenzie's own plain words: "I happened to be walking aft when I saw a big wave break over the stern and smash the cradle. The depth bomb was let loose and started rolling around the decks. I threw myself on it, grabbed it in my arms, braced my feet against the gun platform, and was able to hold it fast."

The bomb was a Sperry depth charge — roughly a hundred pounds of TNT, armed to detonate at fifty feet. The heavy seas washing over the stern made it deadly for anyone even to reach that part of the ship, and there was no way to get help to him until the vessel could be turned into the sea. MacKenzie sat on the live charge and held on, his life depending entirely on how long he could keep his footing against the timbers as each breaker tried to sweep him overboard.

When the charge was finally secured and carried to safety, the crew discovered its safety pin had come detached. Had it kept rolling loose across the deck, the buoy would very likely have separated and the charge exploded, destroying the ship and her some fifty officers and men. As his commander, Lieutenant-Commander Isaac C. Johnson, put it in the official report: "Mackenzie exposed his life and prevented a serious accident to the ship and probable loss of the ship and entire crew."

His "extraordinary heroism in the line of his profession" was made the subject of general orders in the Navy. The Secretary of the Navy, Josephus Daniels, wrote to him directly: "You have upheld the best traditions of the naval service, and the department heartily commends you. The department will also award you a medal of honor." The medal's inscription reads: "Awarded to John Mackenzie, U.S.N.F., for extraordinary heroism, U.S.S. Remlik, December 17, 1917. Saved ship from destruction by securing depth [charge]."

Asked about it later in his small office at Battery Wharf in Boston, MacKenzie brushed the praise aside — "There wasn't much to it; it was simply a case of go get it" — and turned the talk instead to the courage of his officers, "acts of bravery," he said, that "will never be written."

Source: Charles S. Zack, Holyoke in the Great War (1919).
