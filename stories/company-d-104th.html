Holyoke's Own · 26th "Yankee" Division

Where Company D Went

Company D, 104th Infantry — the Holyoke company, from Hampden Plains to the Rhine

Company D of the 104th Infantry was Holyoke's own. It went to France as part of the 26th Division — the Yankee Division, New England's — and it was in the line early, long, and often. Men from this city fill its rolls. This is where the company went, so that when a man's record says he was gassed, or wounded, or that he came home, you can see the ground he was standing on.

September 1917Reorganized at Camp Bartlett, Hampden Plains.
October 1917Left for Montreal, then Halifax, arriving at Liverpool on October 17. Four days later they were across the Channel and on their way to the front. For a time they trained at the village of Sartes.
February 6 – March 21, 1918In the line at the Chemin-des-Dames sector.
Easter Sunday, 1918Arrived in front of Apremont. Held that sector and the one to its right, in front of Montsec, for three months.
Summer 1918Moved to Belleau Wood, and into the fighting at Château-Thierry.
September 11–12, 1918Into the fight at Saint-Mihiel on the 11th; over the top on the 12th. That day they took the town of St. Maurice and advanced five miles in thirty hours.
Autumn 1918After the drive, the few remaining took part in the fighting north of Verdun — going over three times in one day.
Harper records it as a matter of record: of the original 150 men from Holyoke and vicinity who answered the call in April 1917, thirteen were still in the ranks when the Armistice was signed.

A few had been transferred. Most had been killed or wounded. One hundred and fifty Holyoke men went; thirteen were still standing in the ranks at the end. Every man in this archive who wore the Company D badge stood somewhere in that arithmetic.

A word of care: this is the company's history, not automatically any one man's. The men came to Company D at different times — some carried Mexican border service from 1916, some enlisted in the spring of 1917, some were sent as replacements late in the war. Read each man's own entry for the date he entered service, and this page for where his company stood then.
Sources: Wyatt E. Harper, The Story of Holyoke (published for the centennial of the City of Holyoke, 1973), and Charles S. Zack, Holyoke in the Great War (1919).
