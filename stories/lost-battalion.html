Holyoke in the Great War · A Hero's Story

The Lost Battalion

Pvt. Raymond Flynn, Company E, 308th Infantry — the Argonne Forest, October 1918

Private Raymond Flynn of Holyoke, of Company E, 308th Infantry, was with the famous "Lost Battalion" in the Argonne Forest in October 1918. He set down the history of that ordeal himself. His account, in part:

We advanced in the early hours under the most difficult conditions imaginable. No-man's-land was one mass of barbed wire, swamp, and thick underbrush, and a heavy fog lay over it. The attack so surprised the enemy that he had to fall back, and we worked into the trenches he abandoned, resting the first night in mud up to our knees. From the second day on we met heavy machine-gun fire and knew the going would not be easy.

On the night of October 2 we crossed the second hill, and the battalions on our right and left did not keep up. We had started on September 26 with almost 200 men; the strength of E Company on the night of October 2 was exactly 52 men and two officers. That night we walked, unknowing, through the German line and took position on the far side of a valley — while the enemy came in behind us and set his lines on both flanks and on the hill in front.

At daybreak the trap closed. A shower of hand grenades and machine-gun fire came from every side; we were surrounded. For six days and nights we held the hillside, because we could not leave our wounded to fight our way out. It would have taken every unwounded man to carry them, and we were ringed in. There was only one thing to do — hold them off and wait for help. Each day we lost more men, and to hear the wounded crying night after night was enough to take the heart out of any man. I confess I had given up hope of ever seeing Holyoke again.

We sent out patrols every day to try to reach headquarters, and each day they were killed or captured. On the fifth day one of our men came back wounded, carrying a note from the German commander to Major Whittlesey, saying that on the grounds of humanity we should surrender, as they could hear the cries of our wounded every night. I have read many versions of the major's answer in the papers. The truth of it is that he simply remarked to the wounded man who brought the note, "You can tell him to go to hell."

On the sixth day, when the 307th broke through after losing many men, it was like a dream coming true. The Boche made a hasty retreat, and in less time than it takes to tell we had doctors and Red Cross ambulances rushing the wounded and exhausted men back. When I came out I had a two weeks' growth of beard and looked as though I had been drawn through a knot-hole. I was in bed about three weeks; and while I was on my way back to the company, the Armistice was signed — the best bit of news I had ever heard in my young life.

Source: Charles S. Zack, Holyoke in the Great War (1919).
