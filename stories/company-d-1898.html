Holyoke's Own · War With Spain, 1898

San Juan and El Caney

Company D, Second Massachusetts Regiment, United States Volunteers

Twenty years before Company D went to France, it went to Cuba. When war was declared against Spain for the liberation of Cuba, Company D answered for Holyoke — under Captain William J. Crosier, a man the company had elected itself. It was the last of the American wars fought by volunteers, and the last in which military units were permitted to choose their own officers. In Holyoke a fervor that was religious built up as the boys went away.

Cuba

The company was mustered into service on May 3, 1898. On the 21st day of June it landed on Cuban soil and proceeded to Siboney, where it first heard firing in the distance.

The Rough Riders were already in scrimmage with the Spaniards. A horseman appeared, calling out, "Second Massachusetts fall in." The Holyoke boys started up the mountain in single file. That night they camped on the battleground beside the Rough Riders, with the dead lying near. The next day, after a brief resistance, San Juan surrendered.

On the morning of June 27 the company moved into outpost position in full view of Santiago. On July 1 it came into position before El Caney, within range of the guns of the fortress and the target of Spanish sharpshooters in the hills. Here most of the American casualties occurred. With the surrender of El Caney the Holyoke company moved back toward Santiago and was stationed there until the end of the war.

Lieutenants Robert W. Hunter and Francis D. Phillips were the aids of Captain Crosier. A young soldier named Edmund J. Slate became a corporal in Cuba; later he was a general — from Holyoke.

What actually killed them

The American soldiers who went to Cuba had not been trained to conduct a war in the tropics. Lacking proper food and clothing, many became ill. Malaria and yellow fever killed more Americans than did Spanish bullets. Some of the veterans carried the malaria the rest of their lives.

First one, and then another, would be taken sick. No medicine, no hospitals, no care except what comrades gave. The old depot was used as a hospital, and a dirtier place couldn't be imagined. Men lay sick and dying on the floor of that old rookery, and the surgeons were helpless without medical supplies.

The account Harper preserves is of men cleaning up as best they could and keeping to the rules of sanitation as far as they were able — until at last they could boil their water and build beds of bamboo two feet off the ground, if they had the strength for it. And every little while taps would sound, and a volley would be fired over the grave of another man who hadn't made it.
