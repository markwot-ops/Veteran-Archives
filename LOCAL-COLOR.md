# LOCAL COLOR — a research companion for writing narratives

**Purpose.** The archive's narrative standard says: lead with the person, anchor him to Holyoke, give the unit real context, never invent. This file is the raw material for the *anchoring* — what Holyoke was, in the years each man served. Pull from it. Don't paste from it.

**⚠️ COPYRIGHT.** Nearly all of this is drawn from **Wyatt E. Harper, *The Story of Holyoke*** (published for the centennial of the City of Holyoke, 1973), which is **still in copyright**. What follows is deliberately written as **facts in condensed note form, not Harper's prose**. Facts are free; his sentences are not. **Never paste Harper's paragraphs into an entry, a story page, or this file.** By contrast, **Charles S. Zack, *Holyoke in the Great War* (1919), is public domain** and may be quoted freely.

**⚠️ HARPER IS A FINDING AID, NOT A PRIMARY SOURCE.** He is a beloved high-school history teacher writing a popular civic history. He is demonstrably wrong twice already (see PROJECT.md §13C: Elisha Chapin's war, Joseph Morgan's fort). Verify anything load-bearing.

## THE BEGINNING

- **Elizur Holyoke** — the city's namesake. Served his townsmen as lieutenant, finally captain of militia. **Died February 5, 1676, commanding troops in King Philip's War.** Not yet in the archive; no era filter exists for King Philip's War.
- The naming story: two parties of planters explored north, Holyoke up the east bank, Rowland Thomas up the west. They came abreast at the narrows below Hockanum, held a conversation across the water, and each named the mountain at his own feet. Hence Mount Holyoke and Mount Tom.
- **1729** — six families in Ireland Parish. They "forted together" at night for fear of attack.
- **1745** — Benjamin Ball bought a great tract at what is now Northampton and Cherry streets.
- **1749** — **Captain John Miller** settled on the County Road (now Northampton Street); his land covered most of what is now the city. **He had taken part in the capture of Louisburg.** After his army service he married and built a new house. It stayed in the family more than a century, was a **tavern through the Revolution — the half-way house on the stage route between Northampton and Springfield** — and was torn down in 1884.
- **Colonel Ely** kept a tavern in what is now Ingleside. On his farm the last Indian in the locality was permitted to build a hut; unwilling to follow his tribe to new hunting grounds, he lived there several years and died.

## THE REVOLUTION

- **Captain Joseph Morgan** raised a company of recruits that became part of the **3rd Hampshire Regiment**. In it: **Lucas Morgan, Joseph Morgan 2nd, Erastus Morgan** — all Ireland Parish men.
- **Captain Enoch Chapin** commanded a West Springfield company; he too was from Ireland Parish.
- Harper: men from Ireland Parish took part in practically every important engagement of the war.
- **Enoch Ely** fought in the Revolution. The old stories say Shays' Rebellion insurgents, trekking up the valley, **attacked his house** hunting the occupant, who was probably hiding in the neighbouring wood. **For years the doors carried the bullet holes.**
- **Eunice Day** gave her husband **Joel Day** and all five sons — **Joel, Jedediah, Eli, Edward, Robert** — to the Revolution. **The Holyoke chapter of the D.A.R. is named in her honour.** Her men are now in the archive: Elmwood and Smiths Ferry. *A story page is waiting to be written here.*
- **Edward and Robert Day** are named on their father Joel's stone at Elmwood **but are not buried there** — Edward at Troy, N.Y.; Robert at Ticonderoga.
- **1740** — Colonel Benjamin Ely, Captain Joseph Ely and Asahel Chapin walked as far as Feeding Hills to attend a Baptist church. The neighbourhood took the name **Baptist Village**; it is now Elmwood.
- The old Baptist Village burying ground on Northampton Street served the Ireland Parish settlement. Recurring family names: **Ely, Morgan, Day, Ball, Chapin, Rogers, Humestone, Miller, Taylor, Hitchcock, Ives, Sexton.** A Stockwell was buried there in 1768.
- 1786 — Lieutenant John Miller, Lieutenant Charles Ball and Lucas Morgan petitioned the General Court to be set off as a separate parish.

## THE CIVIL WAR

- Fort Sumter fired on; Lincoln called for 75,000 volunteers. Over four years **more than 400 Holyoke soldiers and sailors** answered; **more than 50 died.** All 51 named men are now in the Research Queue (`harper-civil-war-roll`).
- **Captain Myron C. Pratt** owned and ran the **Holyoke Mirror**. One day in **1861** he closed the office and went out to lunch — and before the day was out had enlisted in the **Tenth Massachusetts** and was on his way. **He was killed at Fair Oaks.** The Mirror did not publish again until it passed to the Albee Brothers, who concluded Holyoke did not want a newspaper and quit. **Brother James Albee revived it for a time — until he too enlisted, in the 34th Massachusetts.**
- Newspaper chain of title, for dating a man to a job: Morgan's plant → **Hildreth** (renamed it the *Holyoke Mirror*, January 1854, strict cash policy) → **Lilly and Pratt** → Lilly out to **Wheelock** → Wheelock out to **Pratt**, sole owner.

## THE FIRST WORLD WAR

- **Holyoke put 3,797 men and women into service — perhaps the highest percentage of any city its size in the United States.** Useful scale: the Queue is at ~3,000 and Zack's roster is not the whole city.
- **Company D, 104th Infantry** — the itinerary is on the story page (`stories/company-d-104th.html`). 150 Holyoke men answered in April 1917; **13 were still in the ranks at the Armistice.**
- **Francis C. Heywood** (Croix de Guerre, in the archive) — out of **Cornell** in 1911, joined his father at the **Whitmore Manufacturing Company** and became superintendent. **Sent to the Mexican border with the Massachusetts National Guard in 1916; to France with the AEF in 1917.** **While he was overseas his father, Francis D. Heywood, died**, and the family's interest in Whitmore was sold to ex-Mayor John J. White. Heywood came back to a company his family no longer owned. He was later treasurer and manager of the Marvellum Company, which in 1928 bought the bankrupt Whitmore property — **bringing him back to the same building at 28 Appleton Street.** *His entry has none of this.*

## THE SECOND WORLD WAR

- **About two o'clock Holyoke time, December 7, 1941**, the city learned of Pearl Harbor. **At least one Holyoke boy was on the scene. Four Holyoke boys lost their lives in the attack.** — *Cross-check: the archive holds exactly four men dated Dec. 7, 1941: Gaudrault, Hillman, Leary, Labonte. Independent confirmation.*
- The war was already underway for many: since **1940** men of military age had been registered and called. Holyokers had watched Poland fall, France succumb, the British come home beaten from Dunkirk. **New words entered the Holyoke vocabulary: blitzkrieg, Panzer division, Luftwaffe, Axis.**
- **The home front, concretely:** an air raid organization formed. Blackouts practiced at the wail of a siren. Air raid shelters built. **Air raid spotters stood at the gate house at Ashley Ponds and at Scott Tower.** Wardens learned to put out incendiary bombs. The Red Cross ran first aid and life-saving classes. Women knitted sweaters and wrist bands; girls wrote letters.
- **Westover Field:** the first bombers began rising within months, mostly **B-17s, flown awkwardly by new recruits**. Then one day a whole straggling fleet rose, straightened into a jagged line, and **flew a great circle over Holyoke.** Many Holyoke boys were among those fledgling recruits.
- **Industrial Holyoke:** the **Worthington** plant went to stepped-up night-and-day production. **National Blank Book** turned to war production. Copper and brass were permitted only for munitions. **Unemployment ended.** Women went to the assembly lines under the nickname Rosie the Riveter — **though Harper notes Holyoke women had long worked the textile mills, usually as skilled help.** A profound social change came out of it.
- **The cost:** **over two hundred Holyoke boys killed**, thousands more wounded, many injured for life. **Almost eight thousand Holyoke boys taken from their homes to serve in the army.** — *Cross-check: 197 confirmed / 212 on the city memorial / 211 in Harper. Still unreconciled; see PROJECT.md.*
- A high school teacher's account, preserved by Harper: a boy sat quietly through the class, came to the desk afterward, handed back his book, put out his hand and said goodbye — this was his last lesson, he had enlisted in the Air Force. **For many it was in truth the last lesson.**
- Harper closes the chapter with a paraphrase of the epitaph of the Spartan dead at Thermopylae: <br>**"Stranger go tell at Holyoke that we lie here in obedience to her commands."**

## THE MILLS — for anchoring a man to his work

- **Paper begins in Holyoke with the Parsons Paper Company, 1853.** Founders: **Joseph C. Parsons, Colonel Aaron Bagg, Whiting Street.** They made fine writing papers — letters, notes, blank books, ledgers — which cut against the power company's plan for cotton. Bagg was president for many years.
- **Whitmore Manufacturing Company**, organized in Holyoke **1881** by **Hon. William Whiting** (founder of Whiting Paper), **James D. Whitmore**, and **Francis D. Heywood** — then General Passenger Ticket Agent of the Connecticut River Railroad, working out of the **Cabot Street mill of the Holyoke Water Power Company, the cradle of many Holyoke industries.** Whitmore sold out early; Heywood became treasurer and general manager. Coated papers: color glazes, lithographic plates, railroad blanks, coated bristols.
- **1902** — Crocker-McElwain bought the Cabot Street mill; the tenants had to vacate. Whitmore built a modern coating plant at **28 Appleton Street**. Receivership and liquidation **1928**.
- **Marvellum Company**, incorporated at Holyoke **1921** by **George E. Senseney** (internationally known color etcher, president), **Francis C. Heywood** (treasurer/manager), **Russell S. Bracewell** (clerk/superintendent) — all three out of the **American Writing Paper Company**. Senseney had developed a **flotation, or marbling, process** for decorating paper; the company existed to exploit it. First product **1922: "Marvellustre" cover paper**, then decorated envelope linings (a fad at the time), then fancy box papers. Outgrew **144 Race Street** by 1928 and bought the bankrupt Whitmore property at 28 Appleton Street.

## HOW TO USE THIS

A man's Zack line says he entered service on a date and served in a unit. That is a record, not a life. This file is for the third sentence — the one that puts him somewhere:

- A boy who enlisted in the Air Force in 1942 may well have watched those B-17s circle the city before he ever flew one.
- A man who worked at Whitmore in 1916 and went to the border with the National Guard was working for Francis D. Heywood, whose son went with him.
- A Company D man who entered service in June 1916 carried Mexican border service before France; one who entered in July 1918 did not. **The date in his record decides which paragraph of the company's history is his.**
- **Never write a man into a scene the record doesn't put him in.** Use this to describe the city he left, not to invent what he did.
